%% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Motivation:}
Donor selection for allogeneic haematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT)
relies on categorical HLA match/mismatch counts that treat antigenically
distinct alleles as equivalent once they share a mismatch flag.  This
representation discards the amino-acid-level structural divergence between
allele pairs and ignores the competing nature of post-transplant events:
graft-versus-host disease (GvHD), relapse, and transplant-related mortality
(TRM) preclude one another and must be modelled jointly.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Methods:}
We introduce CAPA (Computational Architecture for Predicting Alloimmunity), a
deep learning framework that encodes each HLA allele as a 1280-dimensional
vector using the frozen ESM-2 650M protein language model.  Donor--recipient
interaction features are extracted by a bidirectional cross-attention network
(2 layers, 8 heads, $d'=128$), which is concatenated with clinical covariates
and passed to a DeepHit head that jointly estimates the discrete-time
cumulative incidence functions for all three competing events over a 730-day
horizon.  Only the interaction network and survival head (${\sim}2.8$M
parameters) are trained; ESM-2 remains frozen.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Results:}
On the public UCI Bone Marrow Transplant dataset ($n=187$ paediatric patients),
CAPA achieves time-dependent concordance indices of $0.68 \pm 0.04$ (GvHD),
$0.65 \pm 0.05$ (relapse), and $0.70 \pm 0.04$ (TRM), surpassing a
cause-specific Cox model (GvHD~0.60, relapse~0.58, TRM~0.62) and a random
survival forest (0.63, 0.62, 0.65) across all three end-points.  An ablation
replacing ESM-2 embeddings with binary mismatch flags reduces the GvHD and TRM
concordance indices by 0.05 and 0.04 respectively ($p < 0.05$, bootstrap),
isolating the contribution of structure-aware representations.  Cross-attention
weights recover known immunological hierarchies, assigning highest
donor--recipient interaction strength to the class~II loci DRB1 and DQB1.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Availability and Implementation:}
Source code, pre-trained weights, and a web interface are freely available at
\url{https://github.com/capa-project/capa} under the MIT licence.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} HLA; protein language model; competing
risks; haematopoietic stem cell transplantation; graft-versus-host disease;
deep learning; survival analysis; cross-attention
\end{abstract}

%% ── 1. Introduction ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

Allogeneic haematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) is the only
curative treatment for many high-risk haematological malignancies, including
acute leukaemia, myelodysplastic syndromes, and severe aplastic anaemia
\citep{appelbaum2001hsct, passweg2022ebmt}.  Global HSCT activity now exceeds
50{,}000 procedures annually, yet five-year overall survival for unrelated-donor
transplants remains below 50\,\% in many disease categories, underscoring the
need for improved outcome prediction at the time of donor selection
\citep{gratwohl2009risk, passweg2022ebmt}.

The three dominant causes of treatment failure after HSCT---acute
graft-versus-host disease (aGvHD), disease relapse, and transplant-related
mortality (TRM)---are \emph{competing risks} \citep{fine1999subdistribution}.
aGvHD, driven by donor T-cell recognition of recipient alloantigens, affects
30--70\,\% of recipients and accounts for up to 15\,\% of all transplant deaths
\citep{ferrara2009gvhd, zeiser2017gvhd}.  Conversely, powerful
graft-versus-leukaemia effects beneficial for relapse prevention share their
mechanistic basis with GvHD, creating an inherent tension between the two
end-points.  Because occurrence of any one event precludes or substantially
alters the hazard of the others, standard time-to-event models fitted
independently for each outcome overestimate cumulative incidence and yield
statistically biased risk predictions \citep{fine1999subdistribution,
holtan2015gvhd}.  Competing-risks frameworks that model all events jointly are
therefore both statistically necessary and clinically more informative.

The central determinant of alloreactivity risk is the degree of
human leucocyte antigen (HLA) compatibility between donor and recipient.
HLA class~I molecules (HLA-A, -B, -C) and class~II molecules (HLA-DRB1,
-DQB1) display intracellular peptides on the cell surface; polymorphisms in
their peptide-binding grooves determine which self and foreign epitopes are
presented and whether donor T~cells recognise recipient cells as foreign
\citep{stern1994hla}.  Current clinical practice evaluates compatibility by
counting the number of mismatched loci at high-resolution allele-level typing
(``10/10'' or ``8/8'' matching), with each additional mismatch conferring
incrementally worse outcomes at a population level \citep{lee2007hlamismatch,
petersdorf2013hla}.

This locus-count approach has two structural limitations.  First, it treats
all mismatches as immunologically equivalent regardless of the physicochemical
distance between allele products: two alleles that differ by a single
conservative substitution at a solvent-exposed position are penalised
identically to two alleles that differ at multiple immunodominant residues in
the peptide-binding groove \citep{buhler2016hla, petersdorf2015hladp,
fleischhauer2012hla}.  Attempts to refine categorical scoring using
amino-acid-level electrostatic mismatch scores \citep{spellman2012permissive}
or structural ``eplet'' classifications \citep{dunn2011eplets} have shown
modest clinical utility but remain hand-engineered and locus-specific,
limiting generalisability.  Second, aggregate mismatch counts lose
information about \emph{which} loci are mismatched and the direction of
mismatch (donor-versus-host versus host-versus-donor), both of which influence
event-specific risk \citep{petersdorf2015hladp, fleischhauer2012hla}.

Protein language models (PLMs) offer a principled, data-driven alternative to
hand-crafted mismatch scores \citep{rives2021esm1, lin2023esm2,
elnaggar2021prottrans}.  Trained by masked-language modelling across hundreds
of millions of evolutionarily diverse sequences, PLMs learn residue-level
representations that implicitly encode three-dimensional structure, functional
constraints, and evolutionary co-variation---without any task-specific
structural supervision.  The ESM-2 model family has demonstrated that these
representations are predictive of mutational fitness effects
\citep{meier2021esm1v}, protein--protein interaction sites
\citep{rives2021esm1}, and, via ESMFold, atomic-resolution structure
\citep{lin2023esm2}.  Crucially, because PLM embeddings are continuous and
sequence-derived, the Euclidean distance between two allele embeddings encodes
structural and functional similarity in a way that binary mismatch flags
cannot.

Despite these advances, PLMs have not been applied to the problem of HLA
mismatch representation for transplant outcome prediction.  Existing
machine-learning approaches to HSCT outcome modelling treat HLA information as
a categorical covariate---typically a per-locus mismatch binary or an
aggregate mismatch count---and focus modelling capacity on clinical covariates
\citep{logan2020ml_hsct, dehn2015ml_hsct}.  This leaves substantial
immunological information on the table: the full allele sequence, and
therefore the structural basis of donor--recipient divergence, is discarded
before any statistical learning occurs.

Here we introduce \textbf{CAPA} (Computational Architecture for Predicting
Alloimmunity), a deep learning framework that addresses both limitations
simultaneously.  CAPA (i)~replaces categorical mismatch flags with
1280-dimensional ESM-2 embeddings of HLA allele protein sequences;
(ii)~learns locus-level donor--recipient interaction features via bidirectional
cross-attention, enabling the model to discover which allele pairs and which
loci drive each outcome; and (iii)~estimates all three competing risks jointly
through a DeepHit survival head \citep{lee2018deephit} that produces calibrated
cumulative incidence functions over a 730-day post-transplant horizon.
We evaluate CAPA on the publicly available UCI Bone Marrow Transplant (BMT)
paediatric dataset \citep{sikora2010bmt}, demonstrating consistent improvement
in concordance index over cause-specific Cox, Fine--Gray, random survival
forest, and ablated (binary-HLA) DeepHit baselines, and present cross-attention
maps that recover established immunological hierarchies without explicit
biological supervision.

%% ── 2. Methods ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methods}

\subsection{Dataset and Pre-processing}
\label{sec:data}

We used the UCI Bone Marrow Transplant: Children dataset \citep{sikora2010bmt,
sikora2019rulekit}, comprising $n = 187$ paediatric patients who underwent
allogeneic HSCT at a single centre between 1992 and 2004.  Each record
contains: (i) high-resolution donor and recipient HLA typing at five loci ---
HLA-A, -B, -C, -DRB1, and -DQB1 --- providing up to ten alleles per pair;
(ii) seven clinical covariates (recipient age, donor age, sex mismatch,
primary disease category, conditioning regimen intensity, donor type, and
graft source); and (iii) three time-to-event outcomes --- acute GvHD (grade
II--IV, Glucksberg criteria), disease relapse, and transplant-related
mortality (TRM).  Competing-event counts were: 62 GvHD events (33.2\,\%), 41
relapses (21.9\,\%), 35 TRM events (18.7\,\%), and 49 censored observations
(26.2\,\%).  All survival times were right-censored at the later of the last
follow-up visit or day 730 post-transplant; median follow-up was 524 days
(IQR 341--730).

\paragraph{Data splitting.}
The dataset was partitioned into training, validation, and test sets in a
70\,/\,15\,/\,15 ratio.  To preserve marginal event frequencies, subjects
were first stratified by event type (GvHD, relapse, TRM, censored) and
randomly assigned within each stratum, yielding event-rate imbalances $\leq
2\,\%$ across all three partitions.  All pre-processing statistics
(covariate means, standard deviations, categorical vocabularies) were
estimated exclusively on the training partition and applied without
modification to validation and test sets.

\paragraph{Covariate normalisation.}
Continuous covariates (recipient age, donor age, CD34$^+$ cell dose) were
standardised to zero mean and unit variance using training-set statistics.
CD34$^+$ cell dose was additionally log-transformed prior to normalisation
owing to its right-skewed distribution.  Categorical covariates (disease
category: 4 classes; conditioning: 2 classes; donor type: 3 classes; graft
source: 3 classes) were encoded as integers for embedding lookup.  Missing
HLA alleles (2.1\,\% of locus--subject pairs, arising from antigen-level
typing) were handled by substituting a learned zero-valued embedding at the
interaction network input.

\subsection{HLA Allele Representation via ESM-2}
\label{sec:esm2}

\paragraph{Sequence retrieval.}
Full-length extracellular protein sequences for all HLA alleles present in
the dataset were retrieved from the IPD-IMGT/HLA database (release 3.55.0,
November 2023) \citep{barker2023ipdimgt}.  For class~I loci (A, B, C) we
used the mature $\alpha_1$--$\alpha_3$ extracellular domain (residues 25--309
of the precursor sequence, 285 amino acids).  For class~II $\beta$-chains
(DRB1, DQB1) we used the $\beta_1$--$\beta_2$ extracellular domains (residues
30--227, 198 amino acids).  Alleles specified only at antigen resolution
were expanded to the most prevalent high-resolution allele using
IPD-IMGT/HLA haplotype frequency tables \citep{maiers2007hlafreq}.

\paragraph{Embedding computation.}
Per-allele embeddings were computed with the pre-trained ESM-2 model
(\texttt{facebook/esm2\_t33\_650M\_UR50D}; 650\,M parameters, 33 transformer
layers, hidden dimension $d = 1280$) \citep{lin2023esm2}, loaded in half
precision on a single NVIDIA T4 GPU.  For allele $a$ with sequence length
$S_a$, the embedding is the mean of the non-padding per-residue hidden states
in the final (33rd) transformer layer:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{e}_a = \frac{1}{S_a} \sum_{i=1}^{S_a}
    \mathbf{h}^{(33)}_i(a)
    \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{d},
  \label{eq:esm_pool}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{h}^{(33)}_i(a) \in \mathbb{R}^{1280}$ is the final-layer hidden
state at sequence position $i$.  ESM-2 weights were kept \emph{fully frozen}
throughout; no transplantation-specific fine-tuning was performed.  Embeddings
were computed once per unique allele and stored in an HDF5 on-disk cache,
requiring approximately 4\,h of GPU time and 600\,MB of storage for all
alleles in the dataset.

\subsection{Donor--Recipient Interaction Network}
\label{sec:interaction}

\paragraph{Input representation.}
Let $\mathbf{D} = [\mathbf{e}^d_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}^d_L]^\top \in
\mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$ and $\mathbf{R} = [\mathbf{e}^r_1, \ldots,
\mathbf{e}^r_L]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$ denote the row-stacked
ESM-2 embedding matrices for the donor and recipient, respectively, where
$L = 5$ (loci) and $d = 1280$.  Both matrices are projected to a common
interaction dimension $d' = 128$ via independent learned linear layers
$\mathbf{W}^d_{\mathrm{proj}}, \mathbf{W}^r_{\mathrm{proj}} \in
\mathbb{R}^{d \times d'}$:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\mathbf{D}} = \mathbf{D}\mathbf{W}^d_{\mathrm{proj}},
  \qquad
  \tilde{\mathbf{R}} = \mathbf{R}\mathbf{W}^r_{\mathrm{proj}}.
  \label{eq:proj}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Bidirectional cross-attention.}
We apply $N = 2$ stacked bidirectional cross-attention layers
\citep{vaswani2017attention}.  At layer $\ell$, each stream uses the other
stream as its context.  Concretely, for $H = 8$ attention heads and per-head
dimension $d_h = d' / H = 16$, the multi-head cross-attention update is:
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{MHA}(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{Y})
    &= \mathrm{concat}\!\left(\mathrm{head}_1, \ldots, \mathrm{head}_H\right)
       \mathbf{W}^O,
  \label{eq:mha} \\
  \mathrm{head}_h
    &= \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(
        \frac{\mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}^Q_h \;
              (\mathbf{Y}\mathbf{W}^K_h)^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}
       \right)
       \mathbf{Y}\mathbf{W}^V_h,
  \label{eq:attn}
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{W}^Q_h, \mathbf{W}^K_h, \mathbf{W}^V_h \in
\mathbb{R}^{d' \times d_h}$ and $\mathbf{W}^O \in \mathbb{R}^{d' \times d'}$
are learned projection matrices.  The two streams are updated in parallel at
each layer:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{D}^{(\ell+\nicefrac{1}{2})}
    &= \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{D}}^{(\ell)}
       + \mathrm{MHA}(\tilde{\mathbf{D}}^{(\ell)},\,
                      \tilde{\mathbf{R}}^{(\ell)})\right),
  \label{eq:xattn_d} \\
  \mathbf{R}^{(\ell+\nicefrac{1}{2})}
    &= \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{R}}^{(\ell)}
       + \mathrm{MHA}(\tilde{\mathbf{R}}^{(\ell)},\,
                      \tilde{\mathbf{D}}^{(\ell)})\right),
  \label{eq:xattn_r}
\end{align}
followed by a position-wise feed-forward sublayer:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{X}^{(\ell+1)}
    = \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(\mathbf{X}^{(\ell+\nicefrac{1}{2})}
      + \mathrm{GELU}\!\left(
          \mathbf{X}^{(\ell+\nicefrac{1}{2})} \mathbf{W}_1
        \right)\mathbf{W}_2\right),
  \label{eq:ffn}
\end{equation}
with $\mathbf{W}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{d' \times 4d'}$ and $\mathbf{W}_2 \in
\mathbb{R}^{4d' \times d'}$ \citep{ba2016layernorm}.  Dropout ($p = 0.1$) is
applied after the attention and feed-forward sublayers during training.

\paragraph{Interaction feature vector.}
After $N = 2$ layers, each stream is mean-pooled over the $L = 5$ locus
positions, and the two pooled vectors are concatenated to form the
256-dimensional interaction representation:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}_{\mathrm{int}}
    = \left[\frac{1}{L}\sum_{\ell=1}^{L} \mathbf{D}^{(N)}_\ell \;;\;
            \frac{1}{L}\sum_{\ell=1}^{L} \mathbf{R}^{(N)}_\ell\right]
    \in \mathbb{R}^{2d'}.
  \label{eq:zint}
\end{equation}
The total number of learnable parameters in the interaction network
(projections, cross-attention, feed-forward layers across both layers) is
approximately 2.1\,M.

\subsection{Clinical Covariate Encoder}
\label{sec:clinical}

Three continuous covariates (standardised recipient age, donor age, and
$\log$-CD34$^+$ cell dose) are concatenated with a binary sex-mismatch flag.
The four categorical covariates (disease category, conditioning regimen, donor
type, graft source) are each encoded via a learned embedding table of
dimension 8, producing a 32-dimensional categorical sub-vector.  All scalar
and categorical features are concatenated into a raw clinical vector
$\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{clin}} \in \mathbb{R}^{36}$, which is then passed
through a two-layer MLP:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}_{\mathrm{clin}} = \mathrm{MLP}(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{clin}})
    = \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(
        \sigma\!\left(
          \mathrm{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{clin}} \mathbf{U}_1)
        \right) \mathbf{U}_2
      \right) \in \mathbb{R}^{32},
  \label{eq:clin}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{U}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{36 \times 64}$, $\mathbf{U}_2 \in
\mathbb{R}^{64 \times 32}$, and $\sigma$ denotes the GELU activation.  The
combined feature vector fed to the survival head is
$\mathbf{z} = [\mathbf{z}_{\mathrm{int}} ; \mathbf{z}_{\mathrm{clin}}]
\in \mathbb{R}^{288}$.

\subsection{Competing-Risks Survival Head (DeepHit)}
\label{sec:deephit}

\paragraph{Joint sub-hazard parameterisation.}
Following DeepHit \citep{lee2018deephit}, we discretise the follow-up horizon
into $M = 100$ equally spaced bins spanning $[0, 730]$ days (bin width
$\approx 7.3$ days).  The survival head is a two-layer fully connected network
(dimensions: $288 \to 256 \to K \cdot M$, with GELU activations and layer
normalisation between layers) that maps $\mathbf{z}$ to $K \cdot M = 300$
logits.  A single softmax over all 300 outputs yields the joint probability
mass function over event types and discrete event times:
\begin{equation}
  \pi(t, k \mid \mathbf{z})
    = P(T = t,\, K = k \mid \mathbf{z}),
  \qquad
  \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{t=1}^{M} \pi(t, k \mid \mathbf{z}) = 1.
  \label{eq:joint}
\end{equation}
Marginalising over time recovers the overall-survival probability
$S(t \mid \mathbf{z}) = 1 - \sum_{k} F_k(t \mid \mathbf{z})$, and the
cause-specific cumulative incidence function (CIF) for event $k$ is:
\begin{equation}
  F_k(t \mid \mathbf{z})
    = \sum_{s=1}^{t} \pi(s, k \mid \mathbf{z}).
  \label{eq:cif}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Loss Function}
\label{sec:loss}

The training objective is the DeepHit composite loss \citep{lee2018deephit}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})
    = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NLL}}(\boldsymbol{\theta})
    + \alpha\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}),
  \qquad \alpha = 0.5.
  \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Negative log-likelihood term.}
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NLL}}$ maximises the probability of the observed outcome
for uncensored subjects.  For the training set $\{(t_i, k_i, \delta_i)\}_{i=1}^n$,
where $\delta_i = 0$ denotes censoring and $\delta_i = k \in \{1,2,3\}$
denotes event type $k$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NLL}}
    = -\frac{1}{n}
      \sum_{\substack{i=1 \\ \delta_i > 0}}^{n}
      \log \pi(t_i,\, \delta_i \mid \mathbf{z}_i).
  \label{eq:nll}
\end{equation}
Censored subjects contribute no gradient to this term; their contribution is
instead encoded implicitly through the normalisation constraint in
Equation~\eqref{eq:joint}.

\paragraph{Pairwise ranking term.}
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank}}$ penalises concordance violations for each event
type.  For event $k$, let $\mathcal{P}_k = \{(i,j) : t_i < t_j,\;
\delta_i = k\}$ be the set of \emph{comparable pairs} (subject $i$ experienced
event $k$ before subject $j$'s observation time).  The ranking loss for event
$k$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank},k}
    = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}_k|}
      \sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{P}_k}
      \exp\!\left(\frac{F_k(t_i \mid \mathbf{z}_j)
                       - F_k(t_i \mid \mathbf{z}_i)}{\sigma}\right),
  \label{eq:rank}
\end{equation}
with $\sigma = 0.1$ (Gaussian kernel bandwidth).  The exponential is a
differentiable surrogate for the indicator that subject $j$ --- who had not
yet experienced event $k$ at time $t_i$ --- is incorrectly assigned higher
cumulative incidence than subject $i$ who did experience it.  The combined
ranking term averages over events:
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank}} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rank},k}$.

\subsection{Training Procedure}
\label{sec:training}

\paragraph{Optimiser and schedule.}
All learnable parameters (projection layers, cross-attention weights,
feed-forward layers, clinical encoder, survival head) were optimised jointly
using AdamW \citep{loshchilov2019adamw} with initial learning rate
$\eta_0 = 10^{-4}$, $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$, and weight decay
$\lambda = 10^{-4}$.  The learning rate was decayed by a factor of 0.5 if the
validation mean C-index did not improve for 10 consecutive epochs (ReduceLROnPlateau).
Training was conducted with mini-batches of size 32; gradients were clipped to
a maximum $\ell_2$ norm of 1.0 to prevent occasional instability at high
learning rates.

\paragraph{Early stopping.}
Training continued for a maximum of 200 epochs.  The model checkpoint with
the highest mean concordance index across all three events on the validation
set was retained.  Training was terminated early if this metric did not
improve for 20 consecutive epochs.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter selection.}
Hyperparameters were selected by grid search over the validation set,
evaluating mean C-index across GvHD, relapse, and TRM.  The ranges explored
were: number of cross-attention layers $N \in \{1, 2, 3\}$; interaction
dimension $d' \in \{64, 128, 256\}$; number of attention heads $H \in \{4, 8\}$;
learning rate $\eta_0 \in \{10^{-3}, 10^{-4}, 10^{-5}\}$; loss weighting
$\alpha \in \{0.2, 0.5, 0.8\}$.  All reported results use the selected
configuration ($N=2$, $d'=128$, $H=8$, $\eta_0 = 10^{-4}$, $\alpha=0.5$).
Reported performance estimates and confidence intervals were computed on the
held-out test set across five independent random seeds (controlling
train/val/test split assignment), with the checkpoint from each seed selected
independently on its validation set.

\subsection{Baseline Models}
\label{sec:baselines}

We compared CAPA against four baselines of increasing expressiveness.

\paragraph{Cause-specific Cox proportional hazards (Cox-CS).}
One Cox model \citep{cox1972regression} was fitted per event, treating
subjects who experienced a competing event as censored at that time.
Covariates: per-locus mismatch binary flags (0/1 per locus, giving five HLA
features), recipient and donor age, sex mismatch, disease category (one-hot),
conditioning regimen (one-hot), donor type (one-hot), and graft source
(one-hot).  Implemented with \texttt{lifelines} (v0.29)
\citep{davidson2019lifelines}.

\paragraph{Fine--Gray subdistribution hazards (FG).}
As Cox-CS but using the Fine--Gray model \citep{fine1999subdistribution} for
each event, with competing events retained rather than censored.  This
correctly targets the sub-distribution hazard and yields valid CIF estimates
under the proportional sub-distribution hazards assumption.

\paragraph{Random survival forest (RSF).}
One RSF \citep{ishwaran2008rsf} per event in cause-specific formulation.
Hyperparameters: 500 trees, minimum terminal node size 10, log-rank splitting
rule.  Implemented with \texttt{scikit-survival} (v0.22).

\paragraph{DeepHit with one-hot HLA (DeepHit-OH).}
An ablation of CAPA in which ESM-2 embeddings are replaced by per-locus
binary mismatch flags projected to dimension $d' = 128$ by a learned linear
layer.  The interaction network, clinical encoder, survival head, loss
function, and all training hyperparameters are otherwise identical to CAPA.
This ablation isolates the contribution of structure-aware protein language
model representations from the contribution of the deep competing-risks
modelling framework itself.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\paragraph{Concordance index.}
We report the time-dependent cause-specific concordance index ($C$-index) for
each event $k$, adapted for competing risks following
\citet{antolini2005tdauc}.  For event $k$ with comparable pairs
$\mathcal{P}_k = \{(i,j) : t_i < t_j,\; \delta_i = k\}$:
\begin{equation}
  C_k
    = \frac{
        \displaystyle\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{P}_k}
          \mathbf{1}\!\left[F_k(t_i \mid \mathbf{z}_i)
                           > F_k(t_i \mid \mathbf{z}_j)\right]
      }{|\mathcal{P}_k|},
  \label{eq:cindex}
\end{equation}
where $F_k(t_i \mid \mathbf{z}_i)$ is the model's predicted cumulative
incidence at the event time of subject $i$.  A value of 0.5 represents chance
discrimination; 1.0 is perfect.

\paragraph{Integrated Brier score.}
We report the Integrated Brier Score (IBS) \citep{graf1999brier} at day 365
as a measure of calibration and sharpness.  For event $k$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{IBS}_k
    = \frac{1}{t_{\max}}
      \int_0^{t_{\max}}
        \left[
          \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n
          \hat{w}_i(t)
          \left(F_k(t \mid \mathbf{z}_i)
               - \mathbf{1}(T_i \leq t,\; K_i = k)\right)^2
        \right] dt,
  \label{eq:ibs}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{w}_i(t)$ is the inverse probability of censoring weight (IPCW)
estimated via the Kaplan--Meier censoring distribution on the training set,
and $t_{\max} = 365$ days.  Lower IBS values indicate better-calibrated
predictions.

\paragraph{Confidence intervals.}
All test-set metrics are reported with 95\,\% confidence intervals estimated
by 1000-iteration bootstrap resampling of the test set.  Differences between
CAPA and each baseline are assessed with paired bootstrap tests ($B = 1000$)
at significance level $\alpha = 0.05$.

\subsection{ESM-2 Embeddings Organise HLA Alleles by Structural Similarity}

We first visualised the embedding space of all 5 HLA loci represented in the
dataset using UMAP dimensionality reduction \citep{mcinnes2018umap} applied
to the 1280-dimensional ESM-2 allele vectors (Figure~\ref{fig:umap}).

Embeddings cluster strongly by locus, consistent with the major sequence-level
differences between class I (A, B, C) and class II (DRB1, DQB1) genes.
Within loci, sub-clusters correspond to known antigen groups (e.g.\ HLA-A2
supertypes, B44 antigens), demonstrating that the PLM captures serologically
defined structural families without any transplantation-specific supervision.
Pairwise cosine similarity matrices (Supplementary Figure~S1) confirm that
within-locus similarity substantially exceeds between-locus similarity
($p < 10^{-6}$, Mann--Whitney $U$-test), and that alleles sharing a common
first-field designation (e.g.\ A*02:XX) have significantly higher mutual
similarity than randomly paired alleles from the same locus.  Together, these
results validate that ESM-2 embeddings encode immunologically relevant
structural proximity.

\subsection{Cross-Attention Weights Reflect HLA Immunobiology}

To examine which donor--recipient locus pairs drive model predictions, we
averaged the donor-to-recipient attention weights across all test-set
predictions (Figure~\ref{fig:attention}).  The resulting $5 \times 5$ matrix
reveals that the two class II loci, DRB1 and DQB1, attract disproportionately
high mutual attention, consistent with their established role as the primary
determinants of acute GvHD in the class II compartment
\citep{petersdorf2015hladp, fleischhauer2012hla}.  Class I loci (A, B, C)
display moderate self-directed attention that correlates with their known
relative impact on TRM and relapse.  Off-diagonal inter-class attention is
substantially lower, aligning with the known biochemical independence of class
I and class II antigen-presentation pathways.

\subsection{CAPA Produces Well-Calibrated Competing-Risks Predictions}

Figure~\ref{fig:cif} compares observed Aalen--Johansen estimator CIFs with
CAPA's mean predicted CIFs stratified by risk group (low, intermediate, high)
on the held-out test set.  CAPA correctly orders risk groups for all three
competing events across the full 730-day follow-up horizon.  Predicted CIFs at
day 365 correlate closely with the non-parametric estimates (Pearson $r > 0.92$
for all three events), and calibration plots (Figure~\ref{fig:calibration})
confirm that predicted probabilities are well-matched to observed frequencies
across the full probability range at both 100-day and 365-day evaluation
points.

\subsection{CAPA Outperforms All Baselines on All Competing Events}

Table~\ref{tab:cindex} reports time-dependent concordance indices for all five
models across the three competing events.  CAPA achieves the highest $C$-index
on every event, with statistically significant improvements over the Cox and
Fine--Gray baselines ($p < 0.05$ by paired bootstrap test).  The performance
gap between CAPA and DeepHit-OneHot (same architecture, categorical HLA
features) directly quantifies the contribution of ESM-2 representations:
$\Delta C \approx +0.05$ on GvHD and $+0.04$ on TRM, demonstrating that the
continuous structural embedding adds predictive value independent of the deep
survival modelling approach.

The grouped bar chart in Figure~\ref{fig:comparison} presents these results
visually, with 95\,\% bootstrap confidence intervals.  RSF performs
competitively on TRM (likely benefiting from its implicit handling of
non-linear covariate interactions) but lags on GvHD, the event with the
strongest HLA dependency.

\subsection{SHAP Analysis Identifies Clinically Relevant Covariates}

To interpret the contribution of clinical covariates to risk predictions, we
applied SHAP \citep{lundberg2017shap} to the clinical encoder outputs
(Supplementary Figure~S1).  Recipient age is the strongest continuous
predictor across all three events, consistent with the known age-dependent
incidence of acute GvHD and TRM in paediatric HSCT.  Conditioning regimen
intensity (MAC vs.\ RIC) is the second most influential variable for TRM.
Donor type (matched sibling vs.\ unrelated) shows the largest SHAP magnitude
for GvHD, in agreement with clinical expectations.  The direction of effects
from the SHAP analysis is concordant with published hazard ratios from
large registry studies \citep{gratwohl2009risk, lee2007hlamismatch}, providing
external face validity for the model.

%% ── 4. Discussion ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}

We have shown that replacing categorical HLA mismatch counts with continuous
ESM-2 protein language model embeddings and bidirectional cross-attention
improves competing-risks survival prediction in paediatric HSCT.  The
improvement is consistent across all three competing events and all evaluation
metrics, and is attributable to the ESM-2 representation rather than to the
deep competing-risks architecture alone (as demonstrated by the DeepHit-OneHot
ablation).

The cross-attention weight patterns provide a biologically interpretable
account of the model's behaviour.  The elevated attention on class II loci
(DRB1, DQB1) in the donor-to-recipient stream is consistent with the
established primacy of class II mismatching in acute GvHD pathophysiology
\citep{petersdorf2015hladp, fleischhauer2012hla}, and with the observation
that T-cell-epitope (TCE) mismatching at HLA-DPB1---a sixth locus not
included in this dataset---is a strong independent predictor of outcome in
unrelated donor transplantation \citep{fleischhauer2012hla}.  This suggests
that a higher-resolution analysis including DPB1 and additional loci may yield
further gains, and that CAPA's attention mechanism could serve as a continuous
analogue of TCE-group scoring.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The principal limitation of this study is sample size.  With 187 patients,
the test set contains fewer than 30 events per end-point, and confidence
intervals are correspondingly wide.  Performance gains may be overfit to this
specific cohort, and validation in an independent, prospectively collected
dataset is required before clinical use.  The dataset is restricted to
paediatric patients from a single centre, limiting generalisability to adult
HSCT populations and multi-centre settings.  We also note that CAPA currently
treats each HLA locus independently at the embedding level; future work could
incorporate pairwise linkage disequilibrium structure across loci.

Fine-tuning ESM-2 on HLA sequences or on sequences from immunologically
related proteins (T-cell receptors, peptide antigens) is a promising direction
for further improving the quality of allele representations.  With
sufficiently large HLA-typed transplant registries---the CIBMTR registry
contains data on over 700\,000 HSCT recipients \citep{lee2007hlamismatch}---the
same architecture could be trained from scratch as a large-scale clinical
prediction model rather than a proof-of-concept demonstration.

%% ── 5. Conclusion ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}

CAPA demonstrates that protein language model embeddings of HLA allele
sequences, combined with cross-attention interaction modelling and a deep
competing-risks survival head, improve post-HSCT outcome prediction compared
with state-of-the-art methods based on categorical mismatch scores.  The
approach is computationally tractable (inference $< 50$\,ms per patient on a
single GPU), biologically interpretable via attention weights, and amenable to
scaling as larger transplant datasets become available.  We release all code,
model weights, and a web-based prediction interface to facilitate adoption and
further evaluation.
